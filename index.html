Navitas

Senior Electrical Engineer (May 2011 - May 2013)

The design and build of Navitas began after Celeritas’s final race in the 2012 Shell Eco-marathon. The team utilized the experience gained from the design and build of Celeritas, to create an all around better car with Navitas. As a senior electrical team member, I mentored and led electrical hardware design, including embedded systems, solar array, wiring, and power train design. Navitas won first place in the 2013 Shell Eco-Marathon with 2,630 MPG-E. We shrank the car, creating less frontal area and used a more aerodynamic integrated fairing design. Weight savings was a key focus in all areas, with a push towards lighter and stronger composite designs in the mechanical systems. Smarter system design and positioning allowed the electrical systems to be much simpler to debug, and the wiring of the car to weigh significantly less.

Eureka! Moment: The Solar Concentrator

One of the most unique parts of the car, the solar concentrator array, happened because of a rule change during the design phase of the car. In the past, there was no size limitation on solar arrays for solar cars. In 2013, Shell revamped the rules, limiting the array to 0.65 square meters for urban concept vehicles and changing the scoring equation. The score was now based on motor energy used minus the solar array energy generated. Meaning if you could somehow generate more energy from your 0.65 square meter solar array than your car used, you could break the scoring calculation. On top of that, our design goal for every car has always been to generate more energy than the car uses, otherwise it's not much of a solar car.

After we received the rule change, we called a meeting to brainstorm ways to meet our design goals. Someone joked about building a giant mirror and sticking it on the car. We realized that it wasn't actually against the rules, and the room froze for seconds. We had our solution, and a year into the design phase, we ended up drastically changing the design of the car.

I led the optical and electrical design of the solar concentrator. We spent a lot of time researching and exploring different design options. We ended up with using a fresnel lens and mirror combination to create a static set of troughs on the back of the car. I used NOAA solar data, and sun position calculators to optimize the capture angle of the array for the dates and location of the Shell Eco-Marathon in 2013. Testing the finalized array in the Indiana sun during March (non-ideal solar conditions) showed the array to output roughly 2.2 times the amount of power of a non-concentrating array.
